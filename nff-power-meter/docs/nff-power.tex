\begin{description}[leftmargin=12mm,style=nextline,itemsep=1mm]
  \item[Note 1 --- the window is the cost, not the packet.] One BLE advertisement is
        \SI{0.99}{\milli\joule}: \emph{eighteen times cheaper} than an MQTT heartbeat. But a
        \emph{delivered} heartbeat means holding an advertising window open long enough for the
        gateway to hear it --- \SI{1.5}{\second}, at \SI{208}{\milli\joule}. \textbf{The radio is
        \SI{7}{\percent} of that; the other \SI{93}{\percent} is simply having the CPU switched on.}
        Optimise the window, not the packet.
  \item[Note 2 --- BLE deletes the SDK's worst battery failure mode.] On WiFi, a device that cannot
        reach its broker retries \emph{forever} on a ladder that saturates at one attempt per minute
        (\texttt{nff\_core.c:31}, hardcoded): \SI{677}{\milli\joule} each, or
        \bad{\SI{500}{\milli\ampere\hour} per month} --- \SI{8}{\percent} of a
        \SI{6000}{\milli\ampere\hour} battery every month, with no telemetry to show for it. An
        advertise-only BLE node \textbf{has no link to fail}: an unheard advertisement costs exactly
        what a heard one costs, and nothing more. \good{This is arguably the strongest
        reliability argument for BLE here, and it is not a power-efficiency argument at all.}
  \item[Note 3 --- BLE OTA costs \num{5.8}$\times$ more, and it \emph{still} does not matter.]
        Once the WiFi OTA row was measured \meas\ rather than modelled, the penalty grew from
        \num{1.29}$\times$ to \bad{\num{5.8}$\times$} --- WiFi turned out to be
        \num{4.2}$\times$ faster than assumed (\SI{122}{\kilo\byte\per\second}, not
        \SI{29}{\kilo\byte\per\second}), and BLE has no such headroom. \textbf{The conclusion
        survives anyway}: \SI{2.40}{\milli\ampere\hour} is \SI{0.04}{\percent} of a
        \SI{6000}{\milli\ampere\hour} battery, and twelve updates a year is
        \SI{30}{\milli\ampere\hour} --- \SI{0.6}{\percent} of the annual budget. \textbf{Ship the
        updates.} A \num{5.8}$\times$ penalty on \SI{0.6}{\percent} is still \SI{0.6}{\percent}.
\end{description}

\section{What this means for a \SI{6000}{\milli\ampere\hour} / 12-month node}

Reference duty cycle: wake every \SI{5}{\minute}, read a sensor, advertise, sleep.
Budget: \SI{5100}{\milli\ampere\hour} usable (\SI{85}{\percent} derate) over \SI{8760}{\hour}
$\Rightarrow$ \textbf{\SI{582}{\micro\ampere} mean}, or \textbf{\SI{48.5}{\micro\ampere\hour} per
cycle}.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{llllX}
\toprule
& \textbf{CPU-active} & \textbf{Sleep floor} & \textbf{$I_\text{mean}$} & \textbf{Life} \\
\midrule
\textbf{D0} \footnotesize naive, devkit  & \SI{31.2}{\second} & \SI{6.5}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{10.8}{\milli\ampere} & \bad{20 days} --- fails on the \emph{sleep floor alone} \\
\textbf{D1} \footnotesize naive, custom board & \SI{31.2}{\second} & \SI{20}{\micro\ampere} & \SI{5.01}{\milli\ampere} & \bad{6.1 weeks} --- the literal spec, built the obvious way \\
\textbf{D2} \footnotesize short wake     & \SI{2.5}{\second}  & \SI{20}{\micro\ampere} & \good{\SI{380}{\micro\ampere}} & \good{18.4 months} --- \textbf{recommended;} no extra parts \\
\textbf{D3} \footnotesize \SI{30}{\second} window, CPU light-slept & \SI{1.2}{\second} & \SI{20}{\micro\ampere} & \good{\SI{394}{\micro\ampere}} & \good{17.7 months} --- $+$\SI{32.768}{\kilo\hertz} crystal \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{center}

\noindent
\fcolorbox{warn}{white}{\parbox{\dimexpr\textwidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule}{%
\textbf{A \SI{30}{\second} \emph{CPU-active} window cannot be made to work, on any radio.} The duty
cycle is \SI{10}{\percent}, so the mean budget of \SI{685}{\micro\ampere} (un-derated) permits at
most \SI{6.85}{\milli\ampere} while awake. \textbf{No ESP32 executes code at
\SI{6.85}{\milli\ampere}} --- Espressif's floor is \SIrange{20}{31}{\milli\ampere} (modem sleep,
\SI{80}{\mega\hertz}, radio \emph{off}). \bad{Over budget by $\geq3\times$ with a perfect battery
and the radio switched off.}

\medskip
\textbf{But ``awake for \SI{30}{\second}'' and ``the CPU runs for \SI{30}{\second}'' are different
requirements}, and they differ by \good{$12\times$} in battery life. D3 keeps the full
\SI{30}{\second} \emph{advertising window} and runs the CPU for only \SI{1.2}{\second} of it, the
rest in automatic light sleep with the BT controller holding the advertising timing.
\textbf{Ceiling: the CPU may run for $\leq\SI{3.9}{\second}$ per \SI{5}{\minute} cycle.}}}

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{The sleep floor is a board property, not a chip property.} A devkit draws
\bad{\SI{6.5}{\milli\ampere} asleep} (AMS1117 \SI{5}{\milli\ampere} $+$ power LED
\SI{1.4}{\milli\ampere} $+$ CP2102) --- \bad{\num{11}$\times$ the entire per-cycle budget, while
doing nothing at all}. A custom board with a low-$I_q$ LDO reaches \good{\SI{20}{\micro\ampere}}.
\textbf{No firmware recovers a devkit, and any battery-life figure measured on one is meaningless.}

\medskip
\noindent
\fcolorbox{warn}{white}{\parbox{\dimexpr\textwidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule}{%
\textbf{Read the table in order.} Only \emph{one} tunable parameter --- \texttt{heartbeat\_interval\_s}
--- moves the needle, and it saves \SI{1419}{\milli\ampere\hour}/yr. \textbf{Every other lever that
matters is in the bottom half of the table, and none of them is a setting.} Two are hardcoded
constants and two are features that do not exist. \bad{A \SI{6000}{\milli\ampere\hour} / 12-month node
cannot be configured out of the current SDK; it has to be built.} That is the finding, and it is why
the SDK work list specifies work rather than settings.}}
