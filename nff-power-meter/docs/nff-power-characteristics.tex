\section{Derivation --- how the BLE column was obtained}
\label{sec:model}

The BLE column is not independently modelled. It is computed from the WiFi column by two ratios,
so that every BLE figure is traceable and falsifiable.

\subsection*{(a) Current --- a two-term model}

The module's active current does \emph{not} scale with the radio, because roughly half of it is not
the radio:
\[
I_\text{total} \;=\; \underbrace{I_\text{core}}_{\text{CPU\,@\,80\,MHz + PLL + flash ctrl}}
\;+\; I_\text{radio},
\qquad
I_\text{core} = \SI{35.0}{\milli\ampere}\ \ \textbf{(\meas\ measured; identical for both radios)}.
\]
\begin{center}
\begin{tabular}{lccc}
\toprule
& \textbf{$I_\text{core}$} & \textbf{$I_\text{radio}$} & \textbf{$I_\text{total}$} \\
\midrule
WiFi, connected idle \meas            & \SI{35.0}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{14.7}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{49.7}{\milli\ampere} \\
WiFi, link bring-up \meas             & \SI{35.0}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{29.7}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{64.7}{\milli\ampere} \\
WiFi, bulk RX / OTA \meas             & \SI{35.0}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{119.6}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{154.6}{\milli\ampere} \\
\midrule
BLE, BT init \footnotesize modelled   & \SI{35.0}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{27}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{62}{\milli\ampere} \\
BLE, advertising \footnotesize modelled & \SI{35.0}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{40}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{75}{\milli\ampere} \\
BLE, OTA \footnotesize modelled       & \SI{35.0}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{73}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{108}{\milli\ampere} \\
\midrule
\multicolumn{4}{@{}l}{\bad{$k_I$ is not one number.} Link bring-up: $62/64.7 = \mathbf{0.96}$. Bulk transfer: $108/150.5 = \mathbf{0.72}$.} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

\noindent
\fcolorbox{warn}{white}{\parbox{\dimexpr\textwidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule}{%
\textbf{Measurement killed the single $k_I$, and it strengthened the thesis.} $I_\text{core}$ is
\meas\,\SI{35.0}{\milli\ampere} --- exactly as modelled, and identical for both radios. But the WiFi
\emph{radio} term is not a constant: it spans \SI{14.7}{\milli\ampere} (connected, idle) to
\SI{119.6}{\milli\ampere} (bulk RX). \textbf{There is no one $k_I$; it must be taken per operation
class.}

\medskip
\textbf{And for link bring-up there is essentially \emph{no} current advantage at all}
(\SI{62}{\milli\ampere} BLE vs \meas\,\SI{64.7}{\milli\ampere} WiFi --- within \SI{4}{\percent}).
The document originally credited BLE with a \num{1.5}$\times$ module-current win. \bad{That win does
not exist.} BLE's \num{12}$\times$ saving on link bring-up is \textbf{entirely time}
(\SI{0.26}{\second} vs \meas\,\SI{3.1}{\second}) --- which is precisely what this sheet has claimed
all along: \textbf{BLE does not win on milliamps. It wins by deleting the protocol.} The
measurement did not weaken that argument; it removed the one part of it that was wrong.}}

\subsection*{(c) The rule --- and the trap}

\begin{center}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{lllX}
\toprule
\textbf{Class of operation} & \textbf{Duration} & \textbf{Current} & \textbf{Energy ratio} \\
\midrule
CPU-bound (ECDSA verify, SHA-256)          & unchanged      & unchanged  & $\mathbf{1.00}$ --- crypto does not care about the radio \\
Protocol/latency-bound (like-for-like handshake) & unchanged & $\times k_I$ & $\mathbf{0.96}$ --- \bad{no meaningful saving} \meas \\
\textbf{Throughput-bound (OTA, crash dump, cert rollover)} & $\times k_t$ & $\times k_I$ & $\mathbf{5.8}$ --- \bad{BLE costs \emph{far} more} \meas \\
\midrule
\textbf{Operations BLE \emph{deletes}} (assoc, DHCP, SNTP, TLS) & --- & --- & \good{$\mathbf{0}$} --- \textbf{this, and only this, is the win} \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{center}

\noindent
\fcolorbox{warn}{white}{\parbox{\dimexpr\textwidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule}{%
\textbf{The trap: you cannot hold duration constant when you change the PHY.} A \SI{1.2}{\mega\byte}
OTA over BLE draws \SI{30}{\percent} \emph{less} current --- for \num{1.9}$\times$ as long. Energy is
the product, so \textbf{a BLE OTA costs \SI{29}{\percent} \emph{more} energy than a WiFi OTA}
($0.68 \times 1.93 = 1.31$). Scaling the current while leaving the time alone yields the opposite
conclusion, and it is the single easiest way to mislead a customer about the heaviest operation in
the system. \emph{(It remains negligible in absolute terms --- \S\ref{sec:ops}, note 3.)}}}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=12mm,style=nextline,itemsep=1mm]
  \item[Note 1 --- the window is the cost, not the packet.] One BLE advertisement is
        \SI{0.99}{\milli\joule}: \emph{eighteen times cheaper} than an MQTT heartbeat. But a
        \emph{delivered} heartbeat means holding an advertising window open long enough for the
        gateway to hear it --- \SI{1.5}{\second}, at \SI{208}{\milli\joule}. \textbf{The radio is
        \SI{7}{\percent} of that; the other \SI{93}{\percent} is simply having the CPU switched on.}
        Optimise the window, not the packet.
  \item[Note 2 --- BLE deletes the SDK's worst battery failure mode.] On WiFi, a device that cannot
        reach its broker retries \emph{forever} on a ladder that saturates at one attempt per minute
        (\texttt{nff\_core.c:31}, hardcoded): \SI{677}{\milli\joule} each, or
        \bad{\SI{500}{\milli\ampere\hour} per month} --- \SI{8}{\percent} of a
        \SI{6000}{\milli\ampere\hour} battery every month, with no telemetry to show for it. An
        advertise-only BLE node \textbf{has no link to fail}: an unheard advertisement costs exactly
        what a heard one costs, and nothing more. \good{This is arguably the strongest
        reliability argument for BLE here, and it is not a power-efficiency argument at all.}
  \item[Note 3 --- BLE OTA costs more, and it does not matter.] \SI{2.40}{\milli\ampere\hour} is
        \SI{0.04}{\percent} of a \SI{6000}{\milli\ampere\hour} battery. Twelve updates a year is
        \SI{30}{\milli\ampere\hour}: \SI{0.6}{\percent} of the annual budget. \textbf{Ship the
        updates.} The \SI{29}{\percent} penalty is real, worth knowing, and irrelevant to the
        decision.
\end{description}

\section{What this means for a \SI{6000}{\milli\ampere\hour} / 12-month node}

Reference duty cycle: wake every \SI{5}{\minute}, read a sensor, advertise, sleep.
Budget: \SI{5100}{\milli\ampere\hour} usable (\SI{85}{\percent} derate) over \SI{8760}{\hour}
$\Rightarrow$ \textbf{\SI{582}{\micro\ampere} mean}, or \textbf{\SI{48.5}{\micro\ampere\hour} per
cycle}.

\begin{center}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{llllX}
\toprule
& \textbf{CPU-active} & \textbf{Sleep floor} & \textbf{$I_\text{mean}$} & \textbf{Life} \\
\midrule
\textbf{D0} \footnotesize naive, devkit  & \SI{31.2}{\second} & \SI{6.5}{\milli\ampere} & \SI{10.8}{\milli\ampere} & \bad{20 days} --- fails on the \emph{sleep floor alone} \\
\textbf{D1} \footnotesize naive, custom board & \SI{31.2}{\second} & \SI{20}{\micro\ampere} & \SI{5.01}{\milli\ampere} & \bad{6.1 weeks} --- the literal spec, built the obvious way \\
\textbf{D2} \footnotesize short wake     & \SI{2.5}{\second}  & \SI{20}{\micro\ampere} & \good{\SI{380}{\micro\ampere}} & \good{18.4 months} --- \textbf{recommended;} no extra parts \\
\textbf{D3} \footnotesize \SI{30}{\second} window, CPU light-slept & \SI{1.2}{\second} & \SI{20}{\micro\ampere} & \good{\SI{394}{\micro\ampere}} & \good{17.7 months} --- $+$\SI{32.768}{\kilo\hertz} crystal \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{center}

\noindent
\fcolorbox{warn}{white}{\parbox{\dimexpr\textwidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule}{%
\textbf{A \SI{30}{\second} \emph{CPU-active} window cannot be made to work, on any radio.} The duty
cycle is \SI{10}{\percent}, so the mean budget of \SI{685}{\micro\ampere} (un-derated) permits at
most \SI{6.85}{\milli\ampere} while awake. \textbf{No ESP32 executes code at
\SI{6.85}{\milli\ampere}} --- Espressif's floor is \SIrange{20}{31}{\milli\ampere} (modem sleep,
\SI{80}{\mega\hertz}, radio \emph{off}). \bad{Over budget by $\geq3\times$ with a perfect battery
and the radio switched off.}

\medskip
\textbf{But ``awake for \SI{30}{\second}'' and ``the CPU runs for \SI{30}{\second}'' are different
requirements}, and they differ by \good{$12\times$} in battery life. D3 keeps the full
\SI{30}{\second} \emph{advertising window} and runs the CPU for only \SI{1.2}{\second} of it, the
rest in automatic light sleep with the BT controller holding the advertising timing.
\textbf{Ceiling: the CPU may run for $\leq\SI{3.9}{\second}$ per \SI{5}{\minute} cycle.}}}

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{The sleep floor is a board property, not a chip property.} A devkit draws
\bad{\SI{6.5}{\milli\ampere} asleep} (AMS1117 \SI{5}{\milli\ampere} $+$ power LED
\SI{1.4}{\milli\ampere} $+$ CP2102) --- \bad{\num{11}$\times$ the entire per-cycle budget, while
doing nothing at all}. A custom board with a low-$I_q$ LDO reaches \good{\SI{20}{\micro\ampere}}.
\textbf{No firmware recovers a devkit, and any battery-life figure measured on one is meaningless.}

\section{What the nff SDK would need}

\noindent
\fcolorbox{warn}{white}{\parbox{\dimexpr\textwidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule}{%
\textbf{The BLE column above describes a device the nff SDK cannot currently build.} Verified by
reading the source, not inferred: \textbf{(1) there is no BLE} --- \texttt{nff\_port.h} is the SDK's
only hardware contract and exposes just \texttt{nff\_port\_mqtt\_*} (MQTT over mTLS) and
\texttt{nff\_port\_https\_get\_stream}; a repo-wide search for \texttt{BLE}, \texttt{NimBLE},
\texttt{bluedroid}, \texttt{esp\_ble\_*} and \texttt{advertis*} returns \textbf{zero hits in any
firmware source}. \textbf{(2) There is no sleep} --- no \texttt{esp\_deep\_sleep},
\texttt{esp\_light\_sleep} or \texttt{esp\_pm\_*} anywhere. The SDK's entire low-power story is one
sentence in \texttt{PROTOCOL.md}: raise \texttt{heartbeat\_interval\_s}.

\medskip
\textbf{The PAL is the clean seam, so this is an addition, not a rewrite:} add
\texttt{nff\_port\_ble\_adv\_*} and \texttt{nff\_port\_sleep\_deep/light()}, an
\texttt{nff\_run\_once()} entry point beside the never-exiting \texttt{nff\_start\_task()},
RTC\_NOINIT state carry (the mechanism already exists in \texttt{nff\_crash.c}), a packed 27-byte
codec, and a gateway-side MQTT bridge.}}

\medskip
\noindent
Two hardcoded behaviours must also become configurable. The \textbf{reconnect ladder}
(\texttt{nff\_core.c:31}) saturates at \SI{60}{\second} and retries forever --- see note 2 above.
And \textbf{SNTP} (\texttt{nff\_port\_esp32\_arduino.c:60}) is unconditional and blocks
\texttt{nff\_connect()} for up to \SI{20}{\second} on every cold boot: on a duty-cycled node that
is \bad{\num{5}$\times$ the entire CPU-active budget, every single wake}.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{Payload constraint.} BLE 4.2 legacy advertising carries \textbf{31 bytes}
(\bad{this part has no extended advertising --- 31\,B, not 255}). After manufacturer-data framing
that is \textbf{27 bytes}. The nff heartbeat is a 250-byte JSON: it \bad{does not fit, by
\num{8}$\times$}, and the gateway must reconstruct it from a packed record
(\texttt{ver}\,1 $+$ \texttt{seq}\,2 $+$ \texttt{flags}\,1 $+$ \texttt{fw\_id}\,2 $+$
\texttt{batt}\,1 $+$ \textbf{sensor 16} $+$ CMAC tag 4 $=$ 27, exactly full).

\noindent
\fcolorbox{warn}{white}{\parbox{\dimexpr\textwidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule}{%
\textbf{The OTA is flash-bound, not radio-bound.} The radio sustains
\SI{419}{\kilo\byte\per\second}; the flash accepts only \SI{154}{\kilo\byte\per\second}. That is
the single most consequential thing the measurement revealed, and it has two edges.
\good{For WiFi it is good news}: the radio has \num{2.7}$\times$ headroom, so the TLS and SHA-256
that a real \texttt{nff} OTA adds --- and which this run did \emph{not} include --- eat radio
headroom, not wall-clock, and the duration should barely move. \bad{For BLE it is bad news}: at
\SI{15}{\kilo\byte\per\second} BLE is radio-bound with no headroom at all, so the $k_t$ penalty
applies in full. \textbf{This is why $k_t$ moved from \num{1.93} to \num{8.1} once the WiFi row
stopped being a mock.}}}

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{What the measurement confirmed.} $I_\text{core} = \SI{35.0}{\milli\ampere}$ against a
modelled \SI{35}{\milli\ampere}, and the OTA mean current \SI{150.5}{\milli\ampere} against a
modelled \SIrange{155}{158}{\milli\ampere}. \textbf{The two-term current model in
\S\ref{sec:model} is sound.} What was wrong was never the currents --- it was the \emph{durations}.

\medskip
\noindent
\textbf{What is still \emph{not} measured}, and must not be read as if it were: every BLE figure
(the SDK has no BLE, so there is nothing to flash); the sleep floors (below this rig's noise ---
\S\ref{sec:measure}); and every \texttt{nff}-SDK-specific operation --- mTLS, MQTT, the heartbeat,
ECDSA verify --- because the workload exercised generic WiFi, HTTP and flash, not the SDK itself.
\textbf{Rig accuracy: relative figures are good to a few percent; absolute figures carry
\SIrange{10}{30}{\percent} because the shunt was never calibrated ($R_\text{eff}$ assumed
\SI{1}{\ohm} nominal; the resistor measures \SI{1.1}{\ohm}).}

\section{Converting this sheet to measured data}
\label{sec:measure}

\noindent
Everything above rests on one quantity: $I_\text{awake}$, the module's mean current with the CPU
running and the radio active. It is tens of \si{\milli\ampere} --- comfortably \emph{inside} the
range of the existing shunt rig (\texttt{datasheet.tex}). One afternoon converts this sheet.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*,itemsep=0.5mm]
  \item \bad{Calibrate --- still outstanding.} Phase 0 has \emph{not} been run, so the rig has no $R_\text{eff}$ and will
        report a confident, wrong number. Run \texttt{WIRECHECK} too --- wired to \emph{nothing},
        the rig once reported a perfectly plausible \SI{590}{\milli\ampere}.
  \item Flash a bare BLE advertiser --- no nff, no WiFi, no sensor. Mount per \texttt{datasheet.tex}
        (\bad{the DUT's USB must not be connected} --- it shorts the shunt).
        \texttt{nff power measure -{}-for 30} $\Rightarrow$ $I_\text{awake}$, and with it the whole
        BLE column.
  \item Repeat with a WiFi/MQTT build $\Rightarrow$ the WiFi column, and the \emph{real} $k_I$,
        which replaces the model in \S\ref{sec:model}.
  \item \bad{The sleep floor cannot be measured on this rig.} At \SI{10}{\micro\ampere} across a
        \SI{1}{\ohm} shunt it is \SI{10}{\micro\volt}, about \num{100}$\times$ below the noise floor
        --- the rig's own datasheet marks deep sleep as out of scope. Use a Nordic PPK2 or an
        INA228. \textbf{Do not read the rig's silence as a zero:} a \SI{6.5}{\milli\ampere} devkit
        floor and a \SI{20}{\micro\ampere} custom-board floor are the difference between 20 days and
        18 months, and the shunt rig returns indistinguishable noise for both.
\end{enumerate}
